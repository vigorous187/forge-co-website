## The Numbers Are Real

2025 was brutal for Canadian restaurants. Closures hit communities across the country at a rate the industry hasn't seen in decades — not pauses, not restructures, permanent shutdowns.

The GTA was hit especially hard. Toronto, Mississauga, Brampton, and the surrounding regions saw hundreds of closures across every cuisine type and price point. Some had been open for decades. Some barely made it past their first year.

This isn't a temporary blip. It's a structural shift in the restaurant industry, and the operators who survive the next few years will be the ones who understand what's changed and adapt their approach to match.

## Why Restaurants Are Closing

The closures aren't caused by any single factor. It's a combination of pressures that squeeze from every direction simultaneously.

**Rising costs across the board.** Food costs have increased 20-25% since 2022 in many categories. Labour costs are up thanks to minimum wage increases across Ontario. Rent in the GTA has been climbing steadily, and insurance premiums have spiked. These costs compress margins that were already thin.

**Consumer spending is shifting.** Inflation-fatigued customers are eating out less frequently and spending less when they do. The average check size has plateaued or declined in real terms for many mid-range restaurants. Fine dining and fast casual have shown more resilience, but the middle of the market is getting squeezed.

**Delivery commission fees.** Third-party delivery platforms take a meaningful cut of every order — enough that restaurants who built their pandemic model around delivery find those margins unsustainable long-term without a direct ordering channel.

**The discovery problem.** This is the factor most restaurant owners underestimate. With 7,000+ restaurants closing, the survivors should theoretically get more customers. But that only works if those customers can find them. The restaurants that invested in digital visibility are absorbing the displaced demand. The ones that didn't are wondering why their dining rooms are still half-empty.

## What Surviving Restaurants Do Differently

After working with restaurants across the GTA, a pattern emerges. The restaurants that are growing through this downturn share three common traits. None of them involve working harder in the kitchen.

### 1. They're Findable Online

When someone in your neighbourhood searches "best [cuisine] near me" or asks ChatGPT for a recommendation, does your restaurant show up? For most restaurants, the answer is no.

Surviving restaurants have invested in being found:

- **Google Business Profile** fully optimized with correct categories, current photos, and accurate hours
- **Google Maps** ranking in the top 3 for their key search terms
- **Review profiles** actively managed across Google, Yelp, and TripAdvisor
- **AI search visibility** so that ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity mention them when asked

This isn't optional anymore. When a competitor down the street closes, the customers who used to go there will search for an alternative. If you're not visible in that search, you don't exist to those customers.

### 2. They Manage Their Reputation Actively

A restaurant at 4.5 stars on Google gets dramatically more clicks than one at 3.8. The difference between those ratings often comes down to whether you're actively managing reviews or ignoring them.

Surviving restaurants:

- **Ask for reviews consistently**, not just when they remember
- **Respond to every review** within 24 hours, positive and negative
- **Monitor review platforms** beyond just Google (Yelp, TripAdvisor, UberEats, DoorDash reviews)
- **Address negative feedback** quickly and professionally, turning potential brand damage into a demonstration of how much they care

Review management isn't vanity. It's a direct revenue lever. A 0.5-star improvement in your Google rating can translate to a measurable increase in walk-in traffic and direction requests.

### 3. They Retain Customers, Not Just Attract Them

Acquiring a new customer costs 5-7x more than retaining an existing one. The math is clear: your existing customer base is your most valuable asset, and the restaurants that survive lean into retention.

What this looks like in practice:

- **Consistent social media presence** that keeps the restaurant top of mind. Not posting for likes, but posting so that when customers think "where should we eat Friday?", your restaurant is the first name that comes to mind
- **Email or SMS for regulars** with genuine value (not spam). A personal invitation to a special dinner, a heads-up about a new menu item, a birthday offer
- **Google Posts and social content** that showcase what's new and give people a reason to come back
- **Community involvement** that builds local loyalty. Sponsoring neighbourhood events, partnering with local businesses, being visibly part of the community

## The Marketing Survival Mindset

Here's the shift that separates growing restaurants from closing ones: **marketing is not a luxury. It's survival infrastructure.**

When costs are rising and margins are shrinking, the instinct is to cut marketing first. It feels like a discretionary expense. But the restaurants that cut marketing during a downturn are the ones that show up in the next closure statistic.

Think about it this way: every month you're invisible on Google Maps, your competitors absorb the customers who would have found you. Every month you're not posting content, your competitors build a stronger relationship with the audience you share. Every month you're ignoring your reviews, your rating slowly declines while theirs improves.

The compounding effect of digital visibility works in both directions. It compounds for you when you invest in it, and it compounds against you when you don't.

## Practical Steps You Can Take This Week

You don't need a massive marketing budget to start protecting your restaurant. Here are five things you can do in the next 7 days:

1. **Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile.** If you've never touched it, this is the single highest-return action you can take. Correct your categories, upload fresh photos, and write a keyword-rich description.

2. **Respond to your last 10 Google reviews.** Positive ones and negative ones. Show Google (and future customers) that you're active and engaged.

3. **Post on Google and Instagram this week.** One photo of your best dish, one behind-the-scenes shot. Don't overthink it. Consistency beats perfection.

4. **Check your listings.** Google your restaurant name and look at what comes up. Are your hours right on Google, Yelp, and TripAdvisor? Is your phone number consistent? Fix any inconsistencies you find.

5. **Set up a review link.** Create a direct link to your Google review page and put it on table cards or receipts. Ask 5 happy customers to leave a review this week.

These five steps cost nothing except time. They won't transform your business overnight, but they'll start building the visibility foundation that separates surviving restaurants from the 7,000 that didn't make it.
